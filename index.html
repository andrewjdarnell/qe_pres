Shared Foundations: What QA and QE Have in Common

Both disciplines exist to reduce risk, protect the customer, and build confidence in the product.

  • Both start from the same skills: understanding requirements, user behaviour, and business context.
  • Both identify risk and likely points of failure, and design practical strategies to reduce them.
  • Both rely on analysis, critical thinking, and communicating issues clearly to stakeholders.
  • Both aim to improve quality, not just detect defects.
  • Automation does not replace that judgment — it extends it, freeing capacity to test more thoroughly where it matters most.

Lots in common — the difference is in how we meet the goal, leveraging technology to prevent defects and shorten the feedback loops.

🤝

Shared Goal

To deliver a reliable, secure product that customers can trust, through rigorous critical analysis.

The Traditional Approach: Quality Assurance (QA)

Reactive testing at the end of the development cycle.

  • Detects defects after they have been introduced, in a separate phase owned by a distinct team.
  • Relies heavily on manual execution and repetitive regression checks.
  • Feedback arrives later — when fixes are most expensive and developer context has been lost.
  • Fosters the perception that quality is "the QA team's responsibility."

This model can work — until delivery speed and product size start to scale.

“We can't release yet, QA is still testing.”

The Evolution: Quality Engineering (QE)

Proactive integration of quality practices throughout the software lifecycle.

  • Applies the same core QA skills continuously, not only at the end.
  • Prevents defects by improving the engineering process itself.
  • Embeds quality checks into CI/CD pipelines, deployment workflows, and daily development habits.
  • Uses automation and data to deliver fast feedback and scale confidence.
  • Preserves human attention for exploratory testing, risk analysis, and high-value judgment.

“Quality is built into the pipeline, not audited at the end.”

Fast Feedback Loops and “Shifting Left”

  • Continuous Feedback: Fast feedback is essential. Automated checks in CI/CD help developers learn quickly and correct issues before they grow.
  • Shifting Left: Enabling better decisions with faster, data-driven feedback — not simply moving test execution earlier.
  • Addressing Complexity: Strong unit, component, and integration tests reduce reliance on slow, brittle end-to-end coverage.
  • Strategic E2E: End-to-end tests remain essential — reserved for critical journeys and system-wide validation.
The cost of a late defect:

Commit 1× → Review 3× → CI 6× → Integration 15× → Staging 40× → Production 100×
Every stage you shift left compounds the saving.

Debunking “Automation Is Too Expensive”

The Economics of Automation

  • Systems are built over years, run for years, and ship again and again — the upfront cost of automation is recovered many times over.
  • The cost of defects escaping into production far exceeds the cost of building reliable checks early.
  • In high-impact systems, risk appetite is often near zero and the cost of failure is unacceptable.
📊

Release Maths — 10-Year System Life

Test-suite runs across build & maintenance phases

Monthly Releases (build: 2 yrs) 2 × 12 = 24
Quarterly Releases (10 yrs) 10 × 4 = 40
Daily Dev & CI Runs (build: 2 yrs, working days) 2 × 220 = 440
Daily Dev & Test Runs (maintenance: 8 yrs, per quarterly) 8 × 4 × 10 = 320
Total Test-Suite Executions 24 + 40 + 440 + 320 = 824

Would you do something manually that you'll run over 800 times?

Why Make the Shift?

For the Business (Managers)

  • More Predictable Projects: Consistent delivery velocity.
  • Faster Time to Market: Automatic regression runs.
  • Higher Confidence: Less risk in every release.
  • Lower Long-Term Costs: Defects found earlier (shift-left) and prevented proactively.

For the Team (QAs)

  • No Scripted Regression: Let machines handle the scripted regression testing — reclaim that time.
  • Upskill into Engineering: Grow coding capability and architectural knowledge.
  • Exploratory Focus: Focus on higher-value tests and exploratory testing.
  • Tool Builders: Build tools that empower the whole organisation.

First Line of Defence

Moving from manual judgement to systematic guardrails:

  • Traditional QA: Manual judgement has to decide what to test and what to skip under time pressure (Risk Based Testing).
  • Quality Engineering: Automation is the first line of defence and the backstop, allowing people to make informed decisions.
Guideline:

The testing you avoid today because of the effort and time it takes is your next automation target.

🛡️

Building Tests That Last

Design for Longevity: The interesting part is not just building tests, but building them so they stay useful as the product changes.

  • Good automation is resilient: designed for maintainability, clarity, and reuse — not just short-term success.
  • A healthy suite grows with the product rather than collapsing under change.
  • Build a durable, maintainable foundation at each level and then extend them.
  • Beg, Borrow, Steal — your colleagues have probably faced similar challenges; kickstart with existing examples.

Durable Automation Foundations

✓ Maintainability & Readability
✓ Adaptability to UI/API modifications
✓ High reusability of testing modules

Action Plan: How Do We Go About This?

Technical Skillset

  • Learn automation skills: Python, browser automation, API testing, and test design.
  • Master the pipeline: CI/CD practices, deployment environments, and observability.
  • Leverage AI thoughtfully: Use it to accelerate scripting while maintaining critical thinking.

Collaborate & Execute

  • Collaborate and ask: Learn from colleagues who have already made the shift.
  • Think big, start small: Automate the highest-value checks first, then expand.
  • Evaluate continuously: Ask whether your feedback loops are fast, useful, and actionable.
